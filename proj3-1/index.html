In this project, I implemented a path-tracing renderer for 3D scenes. I use a Monte Carlo method that simulated the behaviour of light in a scene, working by tracing rays from the camera into the scene, and then bouncing them off surfaces until they hit a light source. Along the way, each ray accumulates colour and light information based on the materials it hits and the lights it bounces off of.

In part 1, I implement the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline. I then sped up rendering in part 2 by implementing an algorithm that constructs a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) that organizes the objects in a scene to speed up rendering. This dramatically reduced the rendering time of even large scenes.

To create realistic lighting effects, I implement two methods for estimating the direct lighting function: uniform hemisphere sampling and importance sampling the lights. In addition, I include global illumination, which accounts for indirect lighting effects. With these two methods, I was able to render realistic looking images of models that use diffuser BSDFs. Finally, to reduce noise while increasing samples per pixel, I implemented adaptive sampling logic, which stops sampling early if the pixel has already converged.

Overall, although this was an extremely long project, it was super interesting and gave me perspective on how realistic rendering effects are achieved. The techniques used in the project to speed up rendering made me think about other ways with which rendering could be accelerated.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

In the context of a path-tracing renderer for 3D scenes, the ray generation and primitive intersection stages are crucial components of the rendering pipeline. The ray generation stage involves casting a ray from the camera into the scene, with the direction of the ray determined by the pixel that is being rendered. This stage is responsible for determining the starting point of the ray, as well as its direction and magnitude.

Once the ray has been generated, it is then passed to the primitive intersection stage. Primitives, such as triangles, boxes, or spheres, are defined mathematically, and by equating their functions with the ray equation, I am able to determine the time, if at all, that they intersect with the ray. This information is then used to calculate the colour and light information for the pixel that the ray corresponds to.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

Using the solution from part 1 requires each ray to check each primitive, which is acceptable for smaller volumes but becomes much too slow as soon as the number of primitives goes above 10k+. To avoid having to check every object, I constructed a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) for each mesh.

The BVH construction algorithm involves recursively partitioning the scene into bounding volumes until each leaf node contains only max_leaf_size primitives. The BVH construction algorithm employs a heuristic known as the splitting point, which is used to determine the best location to split the current BVH node. In my implementation, the splitting point is calculated based on the average of all centroids of the primitives in the current BVH node, and splitting based on the longest axis. (I’m not entirely sure that splitting average centroids is the best approach, I hope to implement a better heuristic in the future). By doing this, the BVH construction algorithm ensures that the partition is relatively balanced, and that each child node has an equal number of primitives, leading to good performance. I also include explicit logic that ensures both partitions must have at least one object to avoid bugs.

To recursively test for intersections in a BVH hierarchy, I first check if the ray intersects the bounding box of the root node of the BVH. If not, I can discard the entire subtree rooted at that node. If the ray does intersect the bounding box, I check if the node is a leaf. If it is, I test the ray against each primitive in the leaf node. If it is not a leaf node, I recursively test the ray against the left and right child nodes, and continue until I reach a leaf node. This process significantly reduces the number of primitives checked for intersection because I can discard entire BVH nodes at a time.

One optimization I made was to not store arrays of primitives in each node of the BVH, rather storing start and stop pointers (which are actually std::vector::iterators) to one contiguous list of primitives. This reduces the amount of memory that the BVH takes up, which increases performance.

By organizing the objects in a scene within a BVH structure, rendering time can be dramatically reduced, especially for larger scenes with many primitives. Below, I show some large volumes, which took only fractions of a second to render (with normal shading again, for now).

Here is a more quantitative analysis. Running -t 8 -r 800 600 -f cow.png ../dae/meshedit/cow.dae (render an 800x600 cow.dae with 8 threads) took 5 seconds with no BVH and 0.0251 seconds with BVH! This amounts to a 99% increase in performance, showing that the use of BVH is very effective for complex models, especially with a good heuristic.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Instead of using plain normal shading, I now use my newfound ray-tracing powers to estimate the direct lighting at each pixel. The direct lighting function can be estimated using two methods: uniform hemisphere sampling and importance sampling the lights. Uniform hemisphere sampling evenly samples the hemisphere and calculates the direct lighting function based on the average of the sample values. Importance sampling the lights divides the light sources based on their intensity and samples based on the probability densities.

To implement direct lighting with uniform hemisphere sampling, I can use a Monte Carlo estimator. I first sample a random direction on the hemisphere above the hit point. I then trace a ray towards the sampled direction and calculate the contribution of the light source by computing the cosine of the angle between the light direction and the normal at the intersection point. I multiply this cosine term with the BSDF value (which, for a diffuser BSDF, is reflectance/πreflectance / \pireflectance/π﻿) and the light's intensity, and divide by the probability density of the direction being sampled (which, for a uniform hemisphere, is 1/2π1/2\pi1/2π﻿). This gives me the direct lighting contribution from the light source for that particular sample. I repeat this process for multiple samples and take the average to get an estimate of the direct lighting function.

Instead of sampling in random directions, importance sampling looks directly at each light in the scene. To implement importance sampling the lights, I loop through each light source. I then sample a point on the chosen light source and calculate the direction from the hit point to the light source, as well as the distance to the light and the probability density of the light being sampled. I then trace a ray towards the light source and calculate the contribution of the light source (if there’s an object between the source and the light, it probably doesn’t contribute very much). I multiple the BSDF value and the light's intensity, and divide by the probability density of the light source being sampled. For both methods, in order to avoid floating-point errors occurring with the ray intersecting the object it originated from, I add an EPS_F offset to the ray’s min_t property. For the light sampling, I also subtract EPS_F from the max_t to prevent the ray from intersecting the light itself (as that obviously doesn’t count as an obstruction).

Overall, importance sampling the lights is able to more accurately capture the lighting of the scene, particularly with point sources of light. Hemisphere sampling struggled to intersect lights that were not oriented directly towards the hemisphere. In addition, hemisphere sampling generally had more noise when rendered with the same parameters—these comparison images were rendered with 64 samples per pixel and 32 light rays.

However, direct light importance sampling is not always free from noise. It is clear that noise is reduced as the number of light rays increases, a difference that is most apparent in the shadows below the bunny’s head. In the 1 and 4 ray renders, the shadow is coarse and harsh. However, by the 64 ray render, the shadow becomes soft and relatively noise-free—much more realistic!

When it comes to accurately capturing the lighting of a scene, importance sampling is generally the preferred method, particularly with point sources of light. Hemisphere sampling can have difficulty intersecting lights that are not directly oriented towards the hemisphere, which can result in more noise in the rendered images. Moreover, hemisphere sampling typically produces more noise when rendered with the same parameters as importance sampling. This is because most of the samples produced by hemisphere sampling do not go towards a light, wasting those rays. On the other hand, importance sampling will actually point a ray towards a light, ensuring that all samples will be taken from angles where the incoming radiance could actually be non-zero. It's important to note, however, that direct light importance sampling isn't always free from noise, and the quality of the rendered image is impacted by the number of light rays used and the number of samples per pixel.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Although direct illumination is more accurate than using normal shading, it is still not very realistic—very rare will you see completely black shadows on objects or a dark ceiling despite a huge area light in a white room. This is because my lighting model only accounts for light that directly enters the camera sensor or bounces once to reach the sensor, when in reality light usually bounces many times before reaching the camera.

Global illumination accounts for indirect lighting effects by simulating the path of light rays as they bounce around the scene. In order to implement global illumination, I first need to trace the path of each ray as it bounces around the scene. This requires me to modify my existing path tracer to allow multiple bounces, rather than just a single bounce.

I implemented a recursive method that accounts not only just for one bounce of light, but subsequent bounces by that ray as well. For a ray that hits an object, I sample a new direction, and beam a new ray in that direction. If that ray intersects another object, I get the radiance of that object and multiply it by the current BSDF and a cosine factor. To account for the Beer-Lambert law, which states that the intensity of light decreases exponentially as it travels through a medium, I also divide by the continuation probability if it is not the first bounce.

To avoid having to trace an infinite number of rays, I need to set a maximum depth for each ray. When a ray reaches max_ray_depth, I stop tracing it and assume that it has no further impact on the scene. This is called the maximum ray depth. In addition, I use a technique called Russian Roulette. Russian Roulette randomly terminates rays with a certain probability (30%), rather than continuing to trace them indefinitely. To implement Russian Roulette, I flip a biased coin with 30% chance. If it doesn’t hit the termination side, I continue tracing the ray. Otherwise, I terminate the ray and return the radiance accumulated so far. I do implement a condition that if max_ray_depth is greater than 1, then there will be at least 1 additional bounce, regardless of Russian Roulette. Therefore, my stopping conditions are:

  1. max_ray_depth reached
  1. we’ve passed the one mandatory additional bounce, and Russian Roulette kicks in
  1. the new ray doesn’t intersect any new object

As you can see, direct illumination alone causes black shadows on the ceiling and on the spheres. Indirect illumination alone removes the black shadows, but does not capture the direct effects of the top light. Combining both direct and indirect illumination results in a more realistic rendering of the scene.

To visualize the effects of max_ray_depth, I rendered CBbunny.dae using different maximum values at 1024 samples per pixel, 16 light rays. In general, the more times the rays are allowed to bounce, the more harsh shadows are reduced and colour from the walls are visible in the shadows of the bunny. Due to the additional radiance modelled, the scene is also generally brighter. However, due to Russian Roulette and the Beer Lambert law, the m=3 and m=100 images are fairly similar. This is because a ray is unlikely to bounce 100 times, and even if it did, the contribution from those bounces are likely to be negligible.

To illustrate the effects of samples per pixel, I rendered wall-e.dae using variable sampling rates and 4 rays at max_ray_depth of 5. It is clear that increasing the number of samples per pixel, which it dramatically increases the runtime cost, results in a significant noise decrease. This is most apparent on the body of wall-e, especially the dark areas near the wheel.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

It is clear that global illumination results in the most realistic and physically-based renders. However, each frame requires anywhere from 30 seconds to 8 minutes of rendering, and even more if we want high resolution images. One idea is to use adaptive sampling: if we know that a pixel is not going to change significantly with more samples, let’s stop sampling that pixel.

To implement adaptive sampling, I first calculate the average illuminance and variance of each pixel. Pixels with high variance or low convergence (determined by maxTolerance) are sampled more. I then sample extra rays in those pixels and combine the results with the previous samples to produce a new estimate of the pixel's colour. I repeat this process until the pixel converges to the desired level of noise—95% confident that the average illuminance is within maxTolerance of the mean—or the maximum number of samples per pixel is reached.
