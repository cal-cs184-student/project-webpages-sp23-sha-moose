Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Cloth simulation involves modelling the behaviour of a cloth as it moves and interacts with other objects in a virtual environment. To do this, I used the Verlet integration method. This method involves calculating the position of each particle at the next time step based on its current position and velocity, as well as the acceleration of the particle due to internal and external forces.

I tried to vary some parameters using scene/pinned2.json. The default parameters result in a relatively realistic cloth dangling from two pinned positions.

When the spring constant ks is small, the cloth appears more fluid and flexible, as it allows the particles to move more freely and stretch further. This is similar to lightweight fabrics such as silk or chiffon, which have a lot of drape and movement. A high ks results in a stiffer and more rigid cloth, as it restricts the movement of the particles and limits how far they can stretch. This is more akin to heavier fabrics such as denim or canvas.

When varying the density, the cloth’s movement changes. The low density cloth acts like a feather, with more fluid more smaller scale movements. Because there is less mass in the same area, the cloth is less responsive to the force due to gravity. In contrast, the higher density cloth is more responsive to gravity, and in the final resting state buckles more due to gravity. As a result, it also had more exaggerated wrinkles.

A low damping value in the cloth simulation results in a cloth that is more fluid and flexible. This leads to more exaggerated movements, as shown in the GIF. A high damping value results in a cloth that is more rigid and less flexible, as the particles and constraints are more strongly affected by damping forces that resist motion. Here, the movement becomes slow and stiff.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

In this section, I implemented collisions with other objects using a collision detection system that applied a correction to the position of the cloth based on the distance between the intersecting points and the surface of the object when there was a collision. I implemented collisions with spheres and planes.

Below, I show a cloth collision with a sphere using different spring constant ks values. If the spring constant of the cloth is low, the constraints between the particles are more flexible and allow for greater deformation of the cloth. As a result, the cloth using ks = 500 clings much closer to the sphere. On the other hand, the cloth using ks = 50000 is much more stiff and maintains its shape more when draped over the sphere.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

In this part, I implemented self-collisions for the cloth using a hashing algorithm to efficiently detect and respond to collisions between nearby particles. This is computationally efficient because it allows for collision detection without comparing all pairs of particles. Below, I show three screenshots that document how the cloth folds on itself.

I repeated this simulation but varied the density and spring constant ks values. The density of the cloth affects its mass and flexibility, and therefore its response to external forces and collisions. A lower density cloth moves more slowly, and also has fewer folds. On the other hand, a higher density cloth is heavier and has much more exaggerated movement.

The spring constant of the cloth affects the stiffness of the constraints between particles, and therefore its internal forces and overall behaviour. A low ks creates a flexible and fluid cloth and a large ks creates a stiffer cloth with significantly fewer folds.

Part 5: Shaders

In this part, I implement several shaders that are used in the cloth simulation. Shader programs are used to control the appearance of 3D objects by defining their lighting, materials, and other visual properties.

A vertex shader is responsible for processing the vertices (or points) of 3D objects, transforming them from their local coordinates to world coordinates, and then projecting them onto a 2D plane for display on the screen. As a result, it can perform transformations on the vertices of the object.

Once the vertices have been transformed by the vertex shader, they are passed on to the fragment shader. The fragment shader is responsible for processing each pixel (or fragment) of the 3D object, determining its colour and other visual properties based on lighting and material information. Thus, the fragment shader can create realistic lighting and material effects, but cannot actually shift the location of anything.

The first shader I implemented is the Blinn-Phong shading model. The Blinn-Phong model calculates the colour of a surface based on three components: ambient, diffuse, and specular. The ambient component is a constant value that represents the general illumination of the scene. It is typically set to a low value, as it is meant to represent the light that is scattered and reflected by the many, but weak bounce light rays in the environment. Here, I have exaggerated the value to make it more visible.

The diffuse component represents the light that is scattered uniformly in all directions by the surface. It depends on the angle between the surface normal and the direction of the light source, as well as the value of the light source and the colour of the surface. It makes up the majority of the lighting in the model. Finally, the specular component represents the light that is reflected in a specific direction by the surface. It contributes to the ‘shiny’ nature of the material.

Extra Credit

For extra credit, I implemented my own shader using principles from all of the above shaders to create a semi-realistic water shader. The shader is a combination of several techniques, including Blinn-Phong shading, bump mapping, reflection, refraction, mirror shading, and texture mapping. I calculate diffuse and specular lighting components similar to Blinn-Phong. This shader uses multiple textures to achieved the desired water appearance. I sample a normal map, which is used to add details. I simulate reflection and refraction using an environment map to sample the surrounding environment, similar to the mirror. I use the Fresnel effect to blend the reflection and refraction contributions.
