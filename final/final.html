Abstract

In this project, we present a novel real-time fluid simulation system that combines the Material Point Method (MPM) with a flexible and user-friendly interface for GPU acceleration. Our system efficiently handles a wide range of fluid behaviors, complex geometries, and user-defined scenarios while providing an interactive environment for simulation control and visualization. Our GUI allows users to interact with the simulation and visualize results in real-time. We demonstrate the system’s capabilities, highlighting its performance and versatility in performing computationally expensive fluid simulations. While our project went through numerous iterations and trials and errors, we were able to successfully implement our system using Taichi, a parallel programming language for high-performance numerical computation.

Technical Approach

Material Point Method-Based (MPM) Fluid Simulation

When considering and designing a method to implement a fluid simulation, we came across multiple approaches. We first started off looking at the Fluid-Implicit-Particle (FLIP) method, which builds upon the Particle-In-Cell (PIC) method for fluid dynamics. As we did more research into various fluid simulation systems, we came across the Material Point Method (MPM). Our MPM implementation handles the simulation of fluid particles, their interactions, and the governing equations solved on a fixed grid. Traditionally, fluid simulations have been performed using either Lagrangian or Eulerian methods, each with their limitations. MPM is a powerful and flexible alternative that combines the strengths of both approaches, offering improved accuracy, stability, and versatility.

The MPM method represents fluid as a collection of particles, each carrying material properties such as position, velocity, mass, and deformation gradient. However, instead of simulating each particle directly, MPM operates on a fixed grid mesh to calculate forces and update particle properties. There are 4 key steps in a MPM step.

  1. Particle-to-grid transfer: Particle properties, including mass and momentum, are extrapolated to the grid nodes
  1. Grid operations: The grid is used to calculate forces, update velocities, and apply boundary conditions
  1. Grid-to-particle transfer: Updated velocities and deformation gradients are transferred back to particles
  1. Particle advection: Particle positions are updated based on their velocities

In order to maintain stability and accuracy in our MPM fluid simulation, we employ substepping, a technique where the simulation updates are divided into multiple smaller time steps within a single frame. This approach allows for better numerical stability and increased accuracy, especially in scenarios with rapidly changing forces or complex fluid behaviors. In our simulations, we employ 25 substeps per frame.

GPU Acceleration using Taichi

The user interface, built using Taichi, allows users to interact with the simulation in real-time, adjust simulation parameters, and switch between different presets. The interface includes options for controlling gravity, particle radius, and material colors. The visualization component renders the fluid particles, allowing users to observe the simulation results in real-time.

Taichi is a high-performance programming language designed for numerical computation. It provides parallel tensors like ti.Vector.field and parallel constructs like ti.kernel (which signifies a compiled function) and ti.grouped (which is similar to #pragma omp for in OpenMP). This allows us to efficiently parallelize particle and grid operations, resulting in a substantial speedup.

While the language shares most of its syntax with Python, Taichi’s just-in-time (JIT) compiler enables the generation of highly optimized GPU code tailored to the specific hardware, ensuring optimal performance. In this project, we make use of its Vulkan bindings, which allow us to take full advantage of the GPU’s capabilities, while maintaining compatibility across a wide range of hardware.

Further, Taichi’s data-oriented design allows us to organize particle and grid data in a memory-efficient manner, reducing memory bandwidth requirements and improving cache utilization. We find that we can fit 65k particles into just ~300MB of system memory, allowing us to scale up our simulations given more GPU compute power.

Fluid-Sphere Interactions

We extend our fluid simulation system with spherical obstacles to create more complex scenarios. We achieved this by extending our MPM solver to handle collision detection and response between fluid particles and spheres. During each substep of the simulation, we identify fluid particles that collide with spherical obstacles. We compute the collision depth and normal for each particle, similar to Project 4: Clothsim. We can adjust the particle’s position to ensure stability and update the particle’s velocity with respect to the surface normal.

Previous Approaches

Position-Based Fluids

As detailed in our Timeline from our initial Project Proposal, our first goal was to get a working, barebones fluid simulator. We decided to work off of Project 4: Clothsim. The major modifications that happened to the project were the structure and simulation of the cloth so that it could better emulate the appearance and movement of a fluid. To do this, we removed the spring system within our cloth grid, and also heavily changed the simulate function by following the Position Based Fluids paper written by Nvidia by Macklin et al. in order to implement such necessary particle movement constraints such as incompressibility correction, viscosity, and vorticity. Since this approach is not the main focus of our project, we will not delve too deeply into the specifics of the computations, but we followed the paper as faithfully as possible. We also updated how we handled collisions to not only properly relocate our particles to the surface of the collision object, but also reflected the particle’s velocity across the collision object’s surface normal to better replicate the behavior of a fluid. The end result was the first draft fluid simulator visualized below. While we did see definite progress in this implementation, we struggled with dealing with the implementation of the cloth’s mesh, which significantly affected our visual output and seemed very difficult to restructure. We hoped to do better, which is what led to trying other approaches to building a fluid simulation.

Simple GLFW Renderer

For the next step of our project, we tried coding a particle based fluid simulator from scratch. We thought that simplifying our code reduced the number of parts that could fail, letting us focus on the theory more. The demo below uses only GLFW and GLAD and implements an all pairs repulsion model where all pairs act like charged particles and repel one another. We also applied dampening and capped out velocities to keep the system stable. Overall, the demo below showed promise but taught us very early on that working with c++ was extremely difficult.

None of us had any prior experience compiling and linking projects of our own since we were always handed the proper CMake files and dependencies before. It was tough to learn how to use C++, how to use CMake, and then how to use OpenGL all in order to reach final goal of drawing a triangle. There was a lot of pre-requisite knowledge we needed before we could do anything with modern OpenGL. Thus, for the next step of our project, we looked for better alternatives.

Results

We demonstrate the capabilities of our fluid simulation system through various custom-built scenes, which are under the “Presets” section in the GUI. In the simulations below, we use a 64x64x64 3D grid, handling 65535 fluid particles at once. When running the simulation on our machines, we average about 13 frames-per-second. For ease of result visualization on this report, all of the simulations are sped up to roughly 220% speed from real-time performance.

Conclusion/Lessons Learned

This project was a difficult but fun experience. We gained valuable insights into different algorithms used in fluid simulations and the tradeoffs associated with each. Although we did not end up using it, we learned a lot about OpenGL and the development workflow associated with C++ (particularly how finicky it is!). Our discovery and experience with Taichi was very positive, and we truly realized the importance of leveraging GPU acceleration, as well as striving for optimization and efficiency, especially in the context of real-time applications. While none of our group members had experience with fluid simulation before, we all learned a lot about fluid dynamics through this project and are excited to apply these lessons to future projects.

In this project, we present a GPU-accelerated MPM fluid simulation system implemented using the Taichi programming language. Our implementation leverages the performance advantages of modern GPUs and provides a real-time and visually engaging environment.
